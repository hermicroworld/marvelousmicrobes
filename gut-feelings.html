The Science Behind This

What your gut is actually doing

🔬
The Microbiome

Your gut hosts ~38 trillion microorganisms — bacteria, archaea, fungi, and viruses. They are not passengers. They ferment your food, train your immune system, and produce compounds that reach your brain. The composition of this community shifts within 24 hours of a dietary change.

⚗️
Short-Chain Fatty Acids

When your gut bacteria ferment dietary fibre, they produce SCFAs — primarily butyrate, propionate, and acetate. Butyrate is the preferred fuel for colonocytes (colon cells) and actively reduces gut inflammation. Low SCFA production is linked to IBD, obesity, and metabolic disease.

🫙
Fermented Foods

Fermented foods like dahi, kimchi, idli batter, and kombucha introduce live microorganisms (mainly Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species) and bioactive compounds into your gut. A 2021 Stanford study found a 10-week high-fermented-food diet significantly increased microbiome diversity.

🧱
The Gut Barrier

The intestinal epithelium is just one cell thick. A healthy mucus layer, maintained by Akkermansia muciniphila and butyrate-producing bacteria, protects this barrier. Ultra-processed foods and excess sugar disrupt this layer — a phenomenon sometimes called "leaky gut" — allowing bacterial products to enter the bloodstream.

🧠
The Gut-Brain Axis

The vagus nerve carries signals between your gut and brain in both directions. Your gut produces ~90% of the body's serotonin. Microbiome composition has been linked to anxiety, depression, and cognitive function — though causality is still being carefully established in human trials.

🌍
Why Traditional Diets Work

Traditional diets — Indian, Japanese, Mediterranean — are rich in fibre, fermented foods, and diverse plant matter. Research consistently shows populations eating traditional diets have higher microbiome diversity than those on Western diets. Diversity is a key marker of gut resilience.
